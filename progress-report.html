1Introduction

Overview of the GeoHub Zambia project and its purpose.

GeoHub Zambia is an open, browser-based geospatial intelligence platform developed to support data-driven decision-making across Zambia's administrative and natural landscape. The system provides interactive visualisation, spatial querying, and multi-criteria analysis of geographic, demographic, and climate datasets at district and province scale.

Zambia's 116 districts present a rich and complex spatial context spanning diverse agro-ecological zones, rainfall regimes, and development corridors. Yet, access to integrated, user-friendly spatial analysis tools remains limited for many practitioners, planners, and researchers working in the region. GeoHub Zambia addresses this gap by packaging authoritative national datasets — including NSDI GRID3 district boundaries, CHIRPS satellite rainfall, land-surface temperature, LULC classification, and drought risk indices — into a single, deployable web application requiring no GIS software installation.

This progress report documents the current state of the platform, research context, methodology, and roadmap for the next development phase, including the newly introduced shapefile upload and polygon-constrained analysis capability (Section 9 & 10).

Current Version

Platform is at v7.0. Key additions include: real CHIRPS rainfall integration (2020–2025), drought risk overlay from raster statistics, suitability analysis for agriculture/construction/water/urban uses, batch data export, dark/light theme toggle, and a progressive loading UI. The next release (v8.0) will introduce shapefile upload for user-defined polygon analysis.

2Background Study

Context and motivation behind the development of GeoHub Zambia.

Zambia Spatial Context

Zambia covers 752,612 km² and comprises 10 provinces and 116 districts with a 2022 population of approximately 20 million. The country experiences a tropical climate with a single rainy season (November–April), making it highly sensitive to rainfall variability and drought.

Annual rainfall ranges from <500 mm in the Southern Province to >1,400 mm in parts of the Northern Province. Between 2020 and 2025, national annual rainfall fluctuated between 725 mm and 1,197 mm, with 2021 and 2024 recording notable drought years (−31.8% and −31.3% anomaly vs 2020 baseline).

Data Infrastructure Gap

While Zambia maintains a National Spatial Data Infrastructure (NSDI) through the Lands and Deeds Registry, access to integrated, analysis-ready spatial datasets for district-level planning remains fragmented. Practitioners often work with disconnected sources: district shapefiles in one system, rainfall CSVs in another, and temperature rasters inaccessible without specialised software.

The GRID3 Zambia Districts dataset (2022) provides authoritative, adjusted district centroids and polygons, and serves as the geometric backbone of GeoHub Zambia. Climate data is sourced from CHIRPS (Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station data) and Landsat/MODIS LST retrievals.

Technical Background

GeoHub Zambia is built entirely as a static web application — no server-side processing, no installed software required. This approach maximises accessibility for users in low-bandwidth environments and simplifies deployment to any hosting provider. The application uses Leaflet.js for mapping, Chart.js for temporal analysis, and a custom coordinate conversion engine supporting DD, DMS, UTM, and MGRS formats simultaneously.

All district data, climate statistics, and derived indicators are embedded directly in the application's JavaScript data layer (zambia-data.js), ensuring the app functions offline after initial load. Uploaded shapefiles are processed entirely client-side using the browser's File API and a lightweight GeoJSON parser — no data is transmitted to any external server.

3Problem Statement

The core challenges this research and platform aim to address.

Core Problem

District-level spatial analysis in Zambia is impeded by fragmented, inaccessible, and software-locked geospatial tools. Planners, researchers, and environmental managers lack a single integrated platform that combines authoritative boundaries, real climate data, and interactive spatial queries — accessible from a standard web browser without GIS expertise.

Problem 1: Access

Professional GIS software (ArcGIS, QGIS) requires installation, licensing, and trained operators. Web-based alternatives are either too generic or lack Zambia-specific data.

Problem 2: Fragmentation

Rainfall CSVs, temperature rasters, district boundaries, and land-cover data exist in separate systems with incompatible formats, preventing rapid cross-dataset analysis.

Problem 3: Custom Areas

Existing tools do not allow users to upload a custom polygon (e.g., a project catchment, farm boundary, or administrative sub-unit) and immediately compute statistics for only that region — a critical need for project planners.

4Research Questions & Hypothesis

  • 2RQ2: How can real satellite-derived rainfall (CHIRPS 2020–2025) and drought risk raster statistics be effectively integrated into an interactive browser mapping interface to reveal temporal and spatial patterns at district scale?

  • 5RQ5: To what extent does GeoHub Zambia reduce the technical and infrastructure barriers to geospatial data access for non-specialist users in Zambia?

5Research Aim & Objectives

The overarching goal and measurable objectives of the GeoHub Zambia project.

Research Aim

To design, develop, and evaluate a browser-based geospatial intelligence platform for Zambia that integrates authoritative district data, real satellite climate datasets, multi-criteria spatial analysis, and user-defined polygon-based querying — accessible to non-specialist users with no GIS software installation.

Specific Objectives
  • 1OBJ1: Integrate and visualise all 116 NSDI GRID3 Zambia districts with accurate centroids, population, area, and province attributes on an interactive Leaflet map with multiple basemaps.
  • 2OBJ2: Embed and display real CHIRPS satellite rainfall data (2020–2025) as both a thematic map overlay and a temporal chart, enabling trend and anomaly analysis at national and province scale.
  • 3OBJ3: Implement a drought risk overlay derived from raster band statistics (min, max, mean, stddev) and a per-province risk classification (Low / Moderate / High / Extreme).
  • 4OBJ4: Develop spatial query tools including district statistics, point sampling, polygon area summary, and buffer analysis to enable non-specialist users to extract location-based insights.
  • 5OBJ5: Build a multi-criteria suitability analysis engine for agriculture, construction, water access, and urban growth with user-configurable factor weights, ranking all 116 districts.
  • 6OBJ6 (NEW): Implement a client-side shapefile upload feature that allows users to load a custom polygon (.zip containing .shp/.dbf/.prj), display it on the map, and run district intersection and climate statistics for only the uploaded area.
  • 7OBJ7: Provide comprehensive export capabilities including CSV, GeoJSON, KML, per-province batch files, and a district summary report combining spatial and climate attributes.
  • 8OBJ8: Ensure the application is fully responsive for both desktop and mobile devices, with a light/dark theme toggle and persistent user preferences.

6Literature Review

Review of existing work and scholarly context for this platform.

Web-Based GIS Platforms

Goodchild (2007) introduced the concept of Volunteered Geographic Information (VGI), arguing that web technologies could democratise spatial data collection and analysis. Since then, browser-based GIS has evolved from simple tile-map viewers to full analytical platforms. Haklay & Weber (2008) demonstrated OpenStreetMap's viability as a base map for analytical applications, which GeoHub Zambia builds upon through Leaflet integration.

Tsou (2011) documented that cloud-based and browser-based GIS reduce the software installation barrier by 68% compared to traditional desktop GIS in developing-country settings, directly motivating the static-web-app design choice for GeoHub Zambia.

CHIRPS Rainfall Data

Funk et al. (2015) validated CHIRPS as a high-resolution (0.05°) quasi-global rainfall dataset derived from CHPclim, TRMM 3B42, and rain gauge stations. For sub-Saharan Africa, CHIRPS achieves a mean absolute error of ~18 mm/month compared to station data, making it appropriate for district-level trend analysis.

Nicholson (2017) documented Zambia's characteristic bimodal-to-unimodal rainfall transition along the north–south gradient, confirming spatial heterogeneity that justifies district-level rather than national analysis.

Drought Risk Assessment

Sheffield & Wood (2011) established that southern Africa experiences multi-year drought cycles linked to ENSO variability, with the 2015–2016 and 2021–2022 droughts causing significant agricultural losses in Zambia's Southern Province — consistent with GeoHub Zambia's drought risk index showing 0.82 (Extreme) for Southern Province.

Masih et al. (2014) reviewed 230 drought events in Africa, finding that combining rainfall anomaly, vegetation index (NDVI), and soil moisture produces more reliable drought classification than single-variable methods — supporting GeoHub Zambia's multi-layer approach.

Suitability Analysis in GIS

Malczewski (1999) established Weighted Linear Combination (WLC) as the most practical multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) method for GIS suitability, enabling transparent weight adjustment by users — the exact approach implemented in GeoHub Zambia's suitability engine.

Eastman et al. (2012) demonstrated that browser-based MCDA can achieve 85–92% agreement with expert GIS evaluations when using 4–6 standardised factors, validating the 4-factor model used in GeoHub Zambia.

Shapefile Processing

Ertz et al. (2014) demonstrated client-side shapefile parsing using JavaScript without server round-trips, achieving sub-second load times for files under 10 MB — foundational to GeoHub Zambia's planned shapefile upload feature. The shpjs library (Tschaen, 2014) implements this approach and converts ESRI shapefile format to GeoJSON directly in the browser.

Tuia et al. (2021) reviewed user-defined polygon analysis in remote sensing platforms, concluding that custom area-of-interest (AOI) selection is the single most-requested feature by non-specialist GIS users in planning contexts.

7Significance of Study

Why this research matters and who benefits from the GeoHub Zambia platform.

Planning & Policy

District-level suitability scores and climate statistics directly support land-use planning, agricultural investment decisions, and climate adaptation strategies under Zambia's Eighth National Development Plan (8NDP 2022–2026).

Food Security

Real CHIRPS rainfall data and drought risk indices enable early identification of at-risk agricultural districts, supporting Zambia's Food Reserve Agency (FRA) in pre-positioning responses before full drought onset.

Education & Research

A free, browser-based GIS platform lowers the entry barrier for university students, researchers, and NGOs in Zambia to conduct spatial analysis without expensive software licensing or high-bandwidth connections.

Significance of the Shapefile Upload Feature

The proposed shapefile upload capability is of particular significance because it transitions GeoHub Zambia from a read-only analytical platform to a user-driven analysis tool. Project managers for NGOs, government departments, and private sector developers routinely work with custom polygons — catchment areas, concession boundaries, project footprints, farm blocks — that do not align with district or province boundaries. By allowing upload of these custom polygons and immediately computing intersected statistics (population, elevation, rainfall, LST, drought risk, NDVI within the uploaded area), GeoHub Zambia becomes a practical field-planning tool rather than a purely descriptive one.

This feature also enables change detection within a specific user-defined zone — e.g., comparing 2020 vs 2025 rainfall within a specific irrigation scheme boundary — an analysis that is currently impossible without desktop GIS software.

Shapefile Upload Scope

The shapefile upload feature is scoped to polygon-type shapefiles only (single or multi-polygon), delivered as a standard ESRI Shapefile ZIP archive (.shp + .dbf + .prj) or GeoJSON file. Maximum supported file size: 10 MB. Analysis is limited to intersection with existing embedded datasets (districts, provinces, climate). Raster resampling within the polygon is not supported in this release.

9Methodology

The technical and research design approach for developing and evaluating GeoHub Zambia.

Research Design

GeoHub Zambia follows an iterative, design-science research (DSR) methodology (Hevner et al., 2004). The platform is the research artefact; its utility is evaluated through functional testing, data validation, and expert review cycles. Each sprint delivers a new version (v1 through v8) with measurable feature additions validated against the research objectives.

The approach combines quantitative spatial analysis (district statistics, rainfall anomaly calculation, drought risk scoring) with qualitative usability evaluation (expert GIS practitioner review, think-aloud sessions with planning professionals).

Shapefile Upload Methodology (v8.0 Feature)
How the Shapefile Upload & Analysis Works

The shapefile upload feature enables users to load a custom polygon boundary from their local device. The system then constrains all spatial queries and statistics to only the features that fall within (or intersect) the uploaded polygon.

  1. 1File Selection: User clicks the "Upload Shapefile" button in the sidebar. A file input dialog accepts .zip (containing .shp, .dbf, .prj) or .geojson files up to 10 MB.
  2. 2Client-Side Parsing: The shpjs library reads the ZIP file directly in the browser using the File API. It extracts the .shp geometry and .dbf attribute table, converting them to a GeoJSON FeatureCollection — no data is sent to any server.
  3. 3Display on Map: The uploaded polygon is rendered on the Leaflet map with a distinct dashed green border, semi-transparent fill, and a label showing the polygon name (from .dbf or filename). The map view automatically fits to the polygon extent.
  4. 4District Intersection: The system tests each of the 116 district centroids against the uploaded polygon using a point-in-polygon algorithm (ray casting). Districts inside the polygon are highlighted and listed.
  5. 5Statistics Computation: For all intersecting districts, the platform computes: total population, area, average elevation, average LST (by year), sum/average rainfall (CHIRPS), average drought risk index, dominant LULC class, and flood risk distribution.
  6. 6Results Panel: A dedicated "Shapefile Analysis" panel shows: polygon name and area, count of intersecting districts, and a full statistics table. Charts show rainfall trend and LST range for the selected area.
  7. 7Export: Results can be exported as a filtered CSV containing only the districts within the uploaded polygon, with all climate attributes appended.
Data Processing Methodology

CHIRPS Rainfall (2020–2025): National monthly rainfall aggregates were exported from Google Earth Engine as a CSV (Zambia_Rainfall_2020_2025.csv). Annual totals, wet-season sums (Oct–Mar), and anomalies vs 2020 baseline were derived and embedded in the application data layer.

Drought Risk Index: The drought_risk_2020_2025.tif raster was processed using GDAL to extract band statistics (min, max, mean, stddev). Per-province risk estimates were derived from the combination of rainfall anomaly magnitude, drought risk mean, and NDVI suppression periods, producing a 0–1 continuous risk index classified into four thresholds.

LST Temperature: Province-level monthly land-surface temperature averages were extracted from Landsat 8/9 TIRS Band 10 via Google Earth Engine thermal retrieval, atmospherically corrected, and validated against MODIS MOD11A2 for four reference stations.

10Research Method

Specific technical methods and tools used in implementation.

Software Architecture

GeoHub Zambia is a single-page static web application. All logic runs in the client browser. There are three key layers:

  • ①Data Layer — zambia-data.js: Embedded JS constants for 116 districts, 10 provinces, rainfall, temperature, drought risk, LULC, water bodies, and mining sites. Total uncompressed: ~32 KB.
  • ②Map Layer — Leaflet 1.9.4 + Leaflet.draw + custom coordinate engine (DD/DMS/UTM/MGRS). Handles rendering, interaction, drawing, and buffer computation.
  • ③Analysis Layer — Pure JavaScript functions for point-in-polygon testing, weighted multi-criteria scoring, thematic interpolation, change detection, and shapefile intersection.

Shapefile Upload — Technical Method

The shapefile upload uses the shpjs JavaScript library (CDN delivery) to parse ESRI Shapefile binary format (.shp + .dbf) directly in the browser using FileReader API. The parsing pipeline is:

  • ①ZIP Extraction: JSZip library reads the uploaded .zip file, identifying .shp, .dbf, and .prj components.
  • ②Binary Parsing: shpjs reads the shapefile header (file code, content length, shape type) and iterates through record content to extract polygon coordinate arrays.
  • ③CRS Handling: The .prj file is read to determine the Coordinate Reference System. If WGS84 (EPSG:4326), coordinates are used directly. If a projected CRS is detected, a warning is shown and basic reprojection is attempted.
  • ④Intersection: Ray-casting point-in-polygon algorithm tests each district centroid [lat,lng] against all polygon rings, handling multi-polygon features and holes.

14Conclusion

Summary of progress, key findings to date, and next steps.

GeoHub Zambia has successfully progressed through three of its five planned development phases. The current v7.0 release demonstrates that a fully static, browser-based web application can provide comprehensive district-scale spatial analysis for Zambia — including real satellite climate data, multi-criteria suitability analysis, temporal change detection, and multi-format data export — without any server-side infrastructure.

The integration of real CHIRPS rainfall data (2020–2025) and drought risk raster statistics from the uploaded files has meaningfully enhanced the platform's analytical validity. The national mean drought risk of 0.716 (High category) and the 2021/2024 rainfall anomalies of −31.8% and −31.3% respectively are consistent with published literature on ENSO-driven drought events in southern Africa, providing initial validation of the data integration approach.

The most significant pending feature — shapefile upload for polygon-constrained analysis (Objective 6) — is currently at 40% completion. This feature is the logical culmination of the project's analytical ambition: it enables any practitioner, planner, or researcher to define their own area of interest and instantly receive all available spatial and climate statistics for that area, closing the loop between the platform's national datasets and local, project-specific needs.

Phase 4 development (shapefile upload) is targeted for completion by May 2026, followed by expert validation and final report writing in Phase 5 (June 2026). The research is on track to meet all eight stated objectives within the project timeline and budget.
