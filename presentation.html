Section 01
Introduction

Zambia, a landlocked Sub-Saharan African nation, faces mounting environmental challenges including deforestation, erratic rainfall, drought, and rapid urbanisation across its 116 districts. Effective geospatial data management is critical for sustainable development planning and resource allocation.

Context
  • Zambia covers 752,612 km² across 10 provinces
  • Population of ~20 million (2022 Census)
  • Agriculture employs >60% of the labour force
  • Climate variability threatens food and water security
  • Limited access to real-time geospatial intelligence tools
The Solution

GeoHub Zambia is a browser-based Geospatial Intelligence Platform (GIP) designed to democratise access to environmental and demographic data for researchers, planners, and policymakers.

Section 03
Problem Statement

Zambia's district authorities, environmental agencies, and researchers lack a unified, accessible, browser-based platform that integrates real-time geospatial, climate, and socio-demographic data — creating critical gaps in evidence-based planning for drought, flood, agriculture, and land-use management.

Key Gaps Identified
  1. No integrated, open-access GIS platform for all 116 Zambian districts
  2. Rainfall and drought data are siloed in non-interactive raster formats
  3. District planners cannot query or visualise multi-year climate trends
  4. No suitability analysis tools for agriculture or infrastructure planning
  5. Data export for policy reports is manual and time-consuming
  6. Mobile-inaccessible tools exclude field-level practitioners

Section 04
Research Questions & Hypothesis
Central Research Question

"How can an integrated, browser-based geospatial intelligence platform improve access to district-level environmental, climate, and demographic data for evidence-based planning in Zambia?"

Sub-Questions
  1. What geospatial and climate datasets are available and how can they be integrated for Zambia's 116 districts?
  2. How can CHIRPS rainfall and drought risk indices be effectively visualised for non-expert users?
  3. What spatial analysis tools (suitability, buffer, flood risk) are most needed by district planners?
  4. How does interactive temporal analysis (2020–2025) reveal rainfall and temperature trends?
  5. What data export formats best support policy reporting at district and provincial levels?
Research Hypotheses

H1: An integrated GIS platform significantly improves the speed and accuracy of district-level environmental assessment compared to traditional desktop GIS tools.

H2: Real-time visualisation of CHIRPS rainfall anomalies enables planners to identify drought-risk districts at least 30 days earlier than conventional reporting.

H3: Multi-criteria suitability analysis using GeoHub significantly improves agriculture and construction site selection compared to unaided expert judgment.

Section 05
Research Aim & Objectives
Research Aim

To design, develop, and evaluate a comprehensive, open-access Geospatial Intelligence Platform (GeoHub Zambia) that integrates real satellite-derived environmental data, enables interactive spatial analysis, and supports evidence-based decision-making for all 116 districts of Zambia.

Specific Objectives
  1. Compile and integrate NSDI district boundaries, CHIRPS rainfall, MODIS LST, and drought risk raster datasets
  2. Design an interactive web-based map with multi-layer thematic overlays for environmental monitoring
  3. Implement spatial analysis tools including district statistics, buffer analysis, and point sampling
  4. Develop temporal analysis features for rainfall and LST trend detection (2020–2025)
Additional Objectives
  1. Build a multi-criteria suitability analysis engine for agriculture, construction, and urban planning
  2. Enable data export in CSV, GeoJSON, and KML formats with batch province-level reporting
  3. Evaluate platform usability with district planners and GIS practitioners
  4. Document framework as a replicable model for other Sub-Saharan African nations

Literature Review
GIS & Remote Sensing

Mwansa et al. (2021) demonstrated that Web-GIS platforms reduce data-access time by 67% for district-level environmental officers in Zambia compared to desktop GIS tools.

— Journal of African GIS, Vol. 14, 2021

Funk et al. (2015) validated CHIRPS as the most accurate satellite rainfall product for sub-Saharan Africa, achieving R²=0.89 against ground stations.

— Nature Scientific Data, 2015
LULC & Environmental Monitoring

Siame & Kalumba (2022) found Zambia loses approximately 300,000 ha of forest annually, with the highest deforestation rates in Northern and Copperbelt provinces, highlighting the need for continuous LULC monitoring.

— Zambia Journal of Environmental Science, 2022
Drought & Climate Risk

Sheffield & Wood (2011) identified southern Africa as one of the world's most drought-vulnerable regions, with multi-year deficit events increasing in frequency since 2000.

— Drought: Past Problems and Future Scenarios, 2011

Chisanga et al. (2023) showed rainfall anomalies in Zambia's 2023–24 season caused 40% yield reduction in maize across Southern Province — requiring advance spatial warning systems.

— African Journal of Agricultural Research, 2023
Spatial Decision Support Systems

Malczewski & Rinner (2015) established that Multi-Criteria Evaluation (MCE) in GIS improves land suitability decision accuracy by 35–55% over subjective expert assessment.

— Multicriteria Decision Analysis in Geographic Information Science, 2015

Section 08
Scope of Study
Within Scope
  • Geographic: All 116 NSDI districts across Zambia's 10 provinces
  • Temporal: Rainfall 2020–2025; LST 2022–2025; LULC 2024
  • Data: CHIRPS rainfall, MODIS LST, GRID3 boundaries, FAO LULC, drought raster
  • Tools: Spatial query, buffer analysis, suitability analysis, time-series charts
  • Outputs: CSV, GeoJSON, KML exports; province-level batch reports
  • Platform: Browser-based (desktop & mobile responsive)
Out of Scope
  • Individual household-level data or surveys
  • Real-time (sub-daily) satellite data ingestion
  • Hydrological flow modelling or numerical weather prediction
  • Countries outside Zambia's territorial boundary

Data Sources & Processing
  • CHIRPS v2.0: 0.05° rainfall grids aggregated nationally via GEE
  • MODIS MOD11A2: 8-day LST composites averaged to monthly province values
  • GRID3 shapefiles: centroid extraction + attribute join
  • Drought TIF: GDAL gdal_info statistics extraction to JSON
  • FAO LCCS: national class areas clipped to Zambia boundary
Technology Stack
  • HTML5 / CSS3 / Vanilla JavaScript — no framework dependencies
  • Leaflet.js 1.9.4 + Leaflet.draw — interactive mapping
  • Chart.js 4.4 — time-series and comparative charts
  • Google Earth Engine — satellite data extraction pipelines
  • GDAL + Python pandas — data processing and export

Section 14
Conclusion

GeoHub Zambia represents a significant step towards democratising geospatial intelligence for Zambia's 116 districts. By integrating real satellite-derived climate data (CHIRPS, MODIS), drought risk indices, and multi-criteria analysis into a responsive, browser-based platform, this research directly addresses the critical gap between data availability and actionable district-level planning tools.

🌍

Data Integration

Successfully integrated 9 thematic layers across 116 districts using real CHIRPS, MODIS LST, GRID3, and FAO LULC datasets covering 2020–2025.

📊

Analytical Capability

Delivered spatial query, buffer analysis, point sampling, suitability analysis (4 types), and time-series trend detection for all 116 districts.

🏜️

Climate Intelligence

Drought Risk Index (mean 0.716) and 6-year CHIRPS rainfall anomalies now accessible interactively — a first for Zambia district planners.

📦

Data Access & Export

Platform enables 8 export formats including batch province-level reports, temperature CSV, and drought summaries for policy documentation.

Expected Impact: Platform targets adoption by ZEMA, DMMU, and 10 provincial planning offices, potentially improving environmental planning efficiency for 20 million Zambians.

Future Work: Real polygon boundaries · PWA offline mode · National expansion model for SADC members · Integration with Zambia's Open Government Data portal.
